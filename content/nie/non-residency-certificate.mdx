<SummaryBox>
The certificado de no residencia (non-residency certificate) is an official document issued by Spain's National Police (Policía Nacional) confirming that the person named in it is not registered as a legal resident of Spain. It is not something most property owners ever need to proactively seek out, but it is occasionally requested by Spanish notaries, banks, and other institutions — particularly in connection with property transactions — to formally confirm your non-resident status.
</SummaryBox>

## What the Certificate Proves

The certificado de no residencia is, in essence, a negative confirmation: it states that the person named is **not** on the Registro Central de Extranjeros (the Central Register of Foreigners, maintained by the Ministerio del Interior) as a legal resident of Spain.

It does not comment on how many days you have spent in Spain, whether you are a Spanish tax resident, or whether you are current with your Modelo 210 filings. It simply confirms residency registration status — a narrow but sometimes important administrative distinction.

<Callout variant="info" title="The certificate proves immigration status, not tax status">
A certificado de no residencia shows you are not registered as a legal resident through the immigration system. It says nothing about whether you are tax-resident for Spanish income tax purposes. These are governed by different rules and different systems.
</Callout>

## Who Issues It

The certificate is issued by the **National Police (Policía Nacional)**, specifically through its Extranjería function. You can request it:

- In person at any Policía Nacional station with an Extranjería unit
- At the main Comisaría in Palma de Mallorca for requests made while in Mallorca
- Via the Spanish government's online Sede Electrónica portal (with a valid Spanish digital certificate or Cl@ve PIN)
- Through a gestoría or authorised representative (using a power of attorney)

It is not issued by the Agencia Tributaria (tax authority), notaries, or local councils — it is specifically a police/immigration certificate.

## When You Might Be Asked for One

Most non-resident property owners in Spain never encounter a specific request for this certificate. However, it does come up in certain situations:

**Property purchases:** Some notaries, especially in transactions involving non-EU nationals, request a certificado de no residencia as part of the documentation bundle to confirm the buyer or seller's tax status for withholding purposes. When a non-resident sells Spanish property, the buyer must withhold 3% of the purchase price and pay it to Hacienda as a security deposit against the seller's potential capital gains tax obligation. Confirming non-resident status is part of managing this obligation correctly.

**Mortgage applications:** Spanish banks occasionally request the certificate when processing mortgage applications from non-resident buyers, as part of their due diligence on the applicant's status.

**Tax filings and refunds:** When filing a Modelo 210 or claiming a refund of non-resident tax, the Agencia Tributaria may request documentation confirming non-resident status.

**Inheritance and estate matters:** Non-resident heirs may need to provide the certificate as part of probate or inheritance tax (Impuesto sobre Sucesiones y Donaciones) proceedings in Spain.

**Banking administration:** Some Spanish banks ask for updated documentation of non-resident status when reviewing accounts under anti-money-laundering or CRS (Common Reporting Standard) protocols.

## How to Obtain One

### In Person in Spain

If you are in Spain, the most straightforward approach is to attend the Extranjería unit of the nearest Policía Nacional comisaría (in Mallorca: Palma de Mallorca) and request the certificate directly.

What you need to bring:
- Your valid passport (original and a photocopy)
- Your NIE number
- In some offices: a completed request form (available at the counter)
- The relevant fee (see below)

In most cases, the certificate is issued on the day or within a day or two.

### Via the Sede Electrónica (Online)

If you have a Spanish digital certificate (Certificado Digital) or are registered for Cl@ve PIN (Spain's digital identity system for dealings with public administration), you can request the certificate through the government's online portal. This is a convenient option for non-residents who are not currently in Spain.

### Via a Gestoría

If you need the certificate but are not in Spain and do not have a Spanish digital certificate, a gestoría or Spanish lawyer can obtain it on your behalf under a power of attorney (poder notarial).

## Cost and Validity

The certificate is subject to a small administrative fee — typically covered under the standard Policía Nacional / Extranjería fee schedule. As of 2026, this is a nominal amount (under €20).

**Validity:** The certificate does not have a statutory validity period in the same way a passport does, but most institutions that request it consider it valid for **three months** from the date of issue. If you obtained one for a property purchase that was then delayed, you may need a fresh one when the deed is eventually signed.

<Callout variant="tip" title="Get it shortly before you need it">
Because most institutions treat the certificate as valid for roughly three months, there is little point obtaining it months in advance. If a notary or bank requests one for a scheduled transaction, obtain it one to four weeks beforehand.
</Callout>

## Common Confusion: Non-Residency Certificate vs Certificate of Fiscal Residency

These two documents are sometimes confused, but they serve entirely different purposes:

If Hacienda is questioning your non-resident tax status, a certificado de no residencia from the police helps, but you will also typically need a certificate of fiscal residency from your home country's tax authority to invoke the double taxation treaty.

## A Note on the Certificado de Residencia

The opposite certificate — a **certificado de residencia** — confirms that you are registered as a legal resident of Spain. Confusingly, this is a different document from the padrón-based empadronamiento certificate. The residencia certificate (also issued by the Policía Nacional) confirms registration on the Registro Central de Extranjeros, while the empadronamiento certificate confirms registration on the padrón municipal. For most practical purposes, institutions accept either as proof of residency, but it is worth confirming which specific document is being requested.
