<SummaryBox>
A Spanish bank account is practically essential for non-resident property owners — you need it for IBI direct debits, utility bills, and community fees. Opening a non-resident account (cuenta de no residente) requires a passport and NIE number. The process takes one to three in-branch visits and typically one to two weeks.
</SummaryBox>

## Why You Need a Spanish Bank Account

Many non-residents initially assume they can manage their Spanish property expenses from a foreign bank account. In practice, this causes recurring problems:

- **IBI (property tax)** and local taxes are collected by direct debit from a Spanish account. Without one, you must pay manually each year — easy to miss, and missing IBI creates enforcement debt
- **Utility contracts** (electricity, water, gas) require a Spanish IBAN for direct debit. Some suppliers refuse to set up contracts without one
- **Community fees** (*cuotas de comunidad*) are typically paid by standing order or direct debit from a Spanish account
- **Inheritance and capital gains tax** payments to the ATIB and Agencia Tributaria are made from Spanish accounts
- **Receiving rental income** — Spanish tenants typically pay rent to a Spanish account

Trying to manage all of these via international transfers from abroad is technically possible but creates significant practical friction and the risk of missed payments.

## Non-Resident vs. Resident Accounts

Spanish banks distinguish between:

- **Cuenta de no residente**: for those who are not Spanish tax residents. Slightly more administrative requirements. The bank is required to report account details to the Spanish tax authority
- **Cuenta de residente**: for Spanish tax residents (those spending more than 183 days per year in Spain, or with their main economic interests here)

As a non-resident property owner, you open a *cuenta de no residente*. The account functions identically from a day-to-day perspective — the main difference is the bank's reporting obligations and the documentation required to open it.

<Callout variant="warning" title="Non-resident accounts and AML checks">
Spanish banks carry out anti-money laundering (AML) checks that are particularly thorough for non-residents. Be prepared to explain the source of funds, particularly if you plan to use the account for a property purchase or receive large transfers. Having documentation ready (proof of income, employment contract, tax returns) speeds up the process significantly.
</Callout>

## What Documents You Need

Requirements vary slightly by bank and can change, but the standard documentation for a non-resident account in Spain is:

- **Valid passport** (original and photocopy)
- **NIE number** — the Spanish tax identification number for foreigners (required; see our buying process guide)
- **Proof of address** in your home country (utility bill, bank statement, local authority registration — typically dated within three months)
- **Proof of income** — payslip, recent tax return, pension statement, or employer letter. The bank needs to understand your economic activity
- **Documentation justifying the account** — for non-residents, banks typically ask about the reason for opening the account (property purchase, rental income management, etc.). Having the nota simple or the purchase deed is useful

Some banks may also ask for a certified copy of the passport translated by a sworn translator, particularly for non-EU nationals.

## The Opening Process

1. **Book an appointment** — do not walk in without one at larger branches. Banks with dedicated non-resident or foreign client teams (ask when calling to arrange an appointment) can manage the full process more smoothly
2. **Attend the branch** in person — at least one visit is required; some banks require two (one to submit documents, one to sign the account opening form)
3. **Complete AML checks** — the bank will carry out identity verification and may ask additional questions about fund sources
4. **Receive account details** — IBAN, internet banking login, and debit card (the card typically arrives within one to two weeks)
5. **Set up direct debits** — once you have the account, contact your utility companies, community administrator, and local tax authority to establish direct debits

The whole process from appointment to functioning account typically takes **one to three weeks**.

## Online Alternatives: Wise and Revolut

**Wise** (formerly TransferWise) and **Revolut** offer multi-currency accounts with Spanish IBANs (*Número de Cuenta Bancaria* compatible). These are useful for:
- Receiving euros and paying some Spanish suppliers
- Avoiding currency conversion fees on small payments
- Situations where you cannot easily visit a Spanish bank branch

**Limitations**:
- Spanish banks and some Spanish direct debit systems do not always accept IBANs from electronic money institutions (Wise and Revolut are licensed as e-money institutions, not banks). IBI direct debits and some community fees have been rejected in practice when set up with Wise/Revolut IBANs
- No Spanish cash withdrawal network
- Not covered by the Spanish Fondo de Garantía de Depósitos (though Wise holds client funds separately from corporate funds; Revolut holds e-money safeguarded funds)

The consensus recommendation: use a traditional Spanish bank account for your core property expenses (IBI, utilities, community fees), and use Wise or Revolut for currency transfer and top-ups.

<Callout variant="tip" title="Keep a buffer in your Spanish account">
Many non-residents set up their Spanish account and then let the balance drop to near zero. This causes failed direct debit attempts, which can result in utility disconnection or community fee arrears. Keep a standing buffer of at least €500–€1,000, and review the account online quarterly.
</Callout>

## What You Cannot Do Without a Spanish Account

To be clear about the practical limitations of operating as a Mallorca property owner without a Spanish bank account:

- Cannot set up IBI direct debit — risk of missed payments and surcharges
- Cannot set up utility direct debits reliably — risk of disconnection
- Cannot receive rental income efficiently
- Cannot make inheritance or capital gains tax payments easily
- Cannot participate in the community's direct debit system for service charges
